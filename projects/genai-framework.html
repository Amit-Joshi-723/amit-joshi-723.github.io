Analytics Architecture Framework · GenAI Products

What to measure when the product itself is AI.

Most GenAI products are instrumented like web apps — sessions, pageviews, time on site. This framework argues that the metric that matters is not whether users stayed. It is whether the output they received was good enough to use.

Built from instrumentation experience across internal AI-powered knowledge tools
Amit Joshi · 2026
Three things this framework gets right that most frameworks miss

Loyal users do not shy away from feedback when something is not right. They are vocal, specific, and honest in a way that no KPI can replicate. If you collect their verbatims systematically and run an LLM over them — clustering emotions, surfacing recurring frustrations, identifying unmet needs — you get a richer picture of product health than any traffic dashboard can give you. Sessions and retention tell you what people did. Verbatims tell you how it felt. Product decisions built on feelings are more durable than product decisions built on click patterns.

01
Measure output quality — not engagement. A user who re-prompts eight times is not engaged. They are frustrated. The tool is failing them.
02
Connect survey verbatims to your zero metrics. Loyal users who fill in surveys are telling you exactly what your dashboard is hiding. Read them. Put an LLM on them. Build from them.
03
Instrument before you build — not after something goes wrong. Without a pre-launch baseline you cannot measure improvement. You can only measure current state.
The problem

You are measuring the wrong thing. And the dashboard looks fine.

When a GenAI product launches, the instinct is to pull the same metrics you use for any web product. Sessions up. Users climbing. Time on site healthy. The product appears to be working. It is not.

Six weeks in, a power user writes in a survey that the outputs keep missing context. Another says they rewrite almost everything before they can use it. None of this shows in the dashboard. Retention is fine. Engagement is high. The product is quietly failing the people who care about it most.

This is the verbatim problem. Loyal users fill in surveys not because they are happy — they do it because they want the product to be better. They are giving you the roadmap. Most teams never read it because it is not in the dashboard.

The second problem is engagement masking failure. When a user submits the same request three different ways trying to get something useful, every attempt registers as engagement. Sessions look rich. The user is struggling. High engagement in a GenAI product is often a frustration signal, not a success signal.

The third problem is timing. Most teams add proper analytics after something goes wrong. At that point there is no baseline. You know the current state but not whether you have improved or declined from where you started. This framework is designed to be set up before launch — not after the first problem appears.

Who this is for

Product owners who inherit the analytics problem after someone else built the tool.

This is for product owners and analytics leads trying to answer — is this product actually working? Are users getting value from the outputs? What are users telling us that the metrics are not? It assumes basic event tracking and access to survey data. It does not assume a data science team. The principles apply whether your tool sits inside a large enterprise platform or runs standalone.

What to measure

Three categories. One central question — did the output work?

Every metric in this framework answers a version of the same question — did the AI output actually help the user do what they came to do? Some metrics answer it directly. Some answer it indirectly through behaviour signals. Some answer it through what users say rather than what they click.

Output quality metrics — the most important category
Output Acceptance Rate OAR
The percentage of AI outputs a user actually uses without significant modification — measured by copy, export, save, or share events fired within 60 seconds of the output appearing on screen. This is your primary health metric. Everything else supports it.
How to capture: fire a "output_accepted" event on copy, export, save, and share. Fire "output_modified" if the user edits before exporting. Calculate OAR as accepted / (accepted + modified + ignored).
Good: above 65%Investigate: below 40%
Prompt Refinement Rate PRR
The percentage of sessions where a user submits more than two prompts for what appears to be the same underlying task. High PRR is a frustration signal — the model is not understanding intent on the first pass. It is not an engagement win. Do not celebrate it.
How to capture: group prompts into tasks using session ID and time proximity. Flag any task group with 3+ prompts as a refinement cluster. PRR = refinement sessions / total sessions.
Good: below 20%Investigate: above 40%
Session Quality Score SQS
A composite score per session that gives you a single health number you can trend over time. A high-quality session has an accepted output, no regenerate clicks, reasonable session depth, and no rage-click signals. A low-quality session has the opposite. Score each session out of 100 and track the weekly average.
Suggested weights: output accepted (40pts) + no regenerate (20pts) + session depth above median (25pts) + no error events (15pts). Adjust weights based on what matters most for your specific product.
Good: above 70Investigate: below 50
Verbatim & sentiment metrics — what your dashboard cannot see
Verbatim-to-Metric Gap VMG
The distance between what survey verbatims say and what your zero metrics show. This is not a number you pull from a dashboard. It lives in a monthly review where someone reads every survey response and asks — does this contradict anything I am seeing in the data? If retention is stable but three power users wrote that outputs are missing context, you have a gap. The gap is your real problem.
How to track: run survey verbatims through an LLM monthly. Cluster by emotion — frustration, confusion, delight, unmet need. Cross-reference clusters against your OAR and PRR trends for the same period. Document where they align and where they diverge.
Good: verbatims confirm metric storyInvestigate: verbatims contradict metrics
Feature Sentiment Score FSS
LLM-derived sentiment score per product feature, extracted from verbatim survey responses. Tells you which features users love, which ones confuse them, and which ones they have stopped mentioning — which is often the most important signal of all. A feature nobody mentions is a feature nobody is using or caring about.
How to capture: tag each verbatim with the feature it references. Run sentiment analysis. Score each feature from -100 (all negative) to +100 (all positive). Track monthly. Any feature below -20 warrants a product review.
Good: above +30 for core featuresInvestigate: below -20 for any feature
Product health indicators — the supporting layer
Intent Cohort Quality Delta ICQD
The difference in SQS between your defined user intent cohorts — power users, explorers, casual users. If your overall SQS looks healthy but your power user cohort is declining, you have a problem your aggregate metrics are hiding. The users who care most are being underserved.
How to segment: tag each session with intent cohort based on entry point, feature used, and session depth. Report SQS by cohort, not in aggregate. A 10-point gap between cohorts is worth investigating.
Good: cohort scores within 10pts of each otherInvestigate: power user cohort declining while overall is stable
Anomaly Detection Coverage ADC
The percentage of metric anomalies that were detected automatically before a human noticed them. This measures the reliability of your monitoring infrastructure, not the product itself. A team that only finds problems reactively — after a complaint, after a visible drop — is always six weeks behind the problem.
How to measure: log every metric anomaly and tag whether it was detected automatically (threshold alert fired) or reactively (human noticed first). ADC = automatic detections / total anomalies.
Good: above 80% detected automaticallyInvestigate: mostly reactive discovery
How it fits together

Four layers. Each one feeds the next.

Skipping a layer does not save time — it means the data exists somewhere but no decision ever gets made from it. The capture layer is useless without processing. The processing layer is useless without storage. And all three are useless if the reporting layer never reaches the person who needs to act on it.

01 · Capture — what to track
Prompt submitted — length, feature context, user cohort tag
Output received — length, type, time to generate
Copy / export / save / share events — the acceptance signals
Regenerate clicks — the frustration signals
Session depth and time — supporting context only
Rage clicks and error events — quality failure markers
Survey responses — verbatims linked to user ID and session
02 · Process — what to calculate
Calculate OAR per session from acceptance events
Flag high-PRR sessions — group prompts into task clusters
Score sessions using SQS composite formula
Tag each session with user intent cohort
Run verbatims through LLM — cluster by emotion and feature
Cross-reference verbatim clusters against metric trends
03 · Store — what to keep
User-level session history — full quality score timeline
Intent cohort membership — track cohort migration over time
Rolling 90-day quality trend — the improvement baseline
Verbatim log linked to user ID — for feature-level sentiment
Pre-launch baseline snapshot — the most important record
Anomaly log — automatic vs reactive detection record
04 · Report — who sees what
Executive view — OAR trend, SQS weekly, verbatim summary
Product team view — cohort quality delta, PRR by feature
Anomaly alert layer — threshold alerts on OAR and PRR
Monthly verbatim review — VMG analysis, feature sentiment
Sprint input — verbatim clusters mapped to backlog items
Tools that fit this architecture: Adobe Analytics or Mixpanel for capture and processing. A data warehouse or flat file store for session history. SSRS, PowerApps, or any BI tool for reporting. An LLM — via API or an internal tool — for verbatim clustering. The last piece is the one most teams never build. It is also the most valuable one.
Five things that go wrong

The mistakes are predictable. That does not make them easy to avoid.

Every mistake in this list was made on a real product. None of them are obvious in the moment because the dashboard always looks plausible. The problem is that dashboards are built to show you what you already decided to measure. They do not tell you what you forgot to ask.

01
Measuring engagement when you should be measuring output quality
Session counts, time on site, and daily active users are reasonable metrics for a content site. For a GenAI product they are actively misleading. When a user submits the same request three different ways trying to get something useful, every prompt registers as engagement. The product looks healthy. The user is quietly deciding never to come back. By the time retention drops, the cause is already three months old.
Replace session count as your headline KPI with Output Acceptance Rate. Move regenerate clicks from the engagement column to the frustration column in your reporting. Brief every stakeholder on why these two metrics tell a different story than the ones they are used to seeing.
02
Leaving survey verbatims in a spreadsheet nobody reads
Loyal users who take the time to fill in a survey are your most honest signal. They are not filling it in because they are happy — they are filling it in because they want the product to be better. In a knowledge tool deployment, retention metrics were stable for three months while power users were repeatedly writing that outputs were missing document context and requiring heavy manual correction. The dashboard said healthy. The verbatims said broken. Nobody connected the two until a senior stakeholder asked a question that nobody could answer from the dashboard alone.
Run verbatims through an LLM monthly. Cluster by emotion and feature. Cross-reference clusters against your OAR and PRR trends for the same period. Present the Verbatim-to-Metric Gap in every product review — not as a footnote but as a first-class input alongside the quantitative metrics.
03
Instrumenting after launch because there was no time before
There is always a reason to skip pre-launch instrumentation. The sprint is full. The tool needs to ship. Analytics can be added later. Later arrives when something goes wrong and the first question anyone asks is — is this better or worse than it used to be? Without a pre-launch baseline, that question has no answer. You know the current state but not the direction of travel.
Define your OAR target, PRR threshold, and SQS formula before the first user touches the product. Instrument during development. Capture a week-one baseline snapshot on day one of launch. This is not optional — it is the only way to measure improvement rather than just current state.
04
Reporting aggregate metrics when cohort metrics tell a different story
An overall SQS of 68 looks healthy. But if your power user cohort is sitting at 52 while casual users are at 78, the product is failing the people who understand it best and rely on it most. Power users tolerate poor quality for longer before they leave — and when they leave, they take their network with them. Aggregate metrics hide this until it is too late.
Segment every quality metric by user intent cohort from day one. Report cohort metrics alongside aggregate metrics in every product review. If any cohort's quality score drops more than 10 points below the overall average, treat it as a priority incident regardless of how the headline number looks.
05
Only finding problems when someone complains about them
Reactive discovery means you are always behind the problem. By the time a stakeholder notices a drop, the issue has usually been running for two to four weeks. The root cause is harder to isolate because you do not know exactly when it started. The fix takes longer because trust has already been damaged.
Set threshold alerts on OAR, PRR, and SQS from day one. If OAR drops more than 10 points week-on-week, alert automatically before anyone notices. Track your Anomaly Detection Coverage as a metric in its own right — the goal is for more than 80% of issues to be detected automatically, not reactively.
Before you go live

The checklist that most teams skip. And later wish they hadn't.

This is not a technical instrumentation checklist — your engineering team handles the event firing. This is the product and analytics decisions that need to be made before a single user touches the tool. Most teams make these decisions retrospectively, when a problem has already appeared. Making them upfront takes two hours. Making them retrospectively takes two months.

Before build — decisions that cannot wait
Define your OAR target
Agree with stakeholders what percentage of outputs should be accepted without modification. Write it down. This becomes your launch success criterion — not session count, not DAU.
Define your PRR threshold
Agree at what re-prompt rate you consider the model to be failing on first-pass intent. Above this threshold, the product team is notified regardless of how other metrics look.
Map your user intent cohorts
Define at least three user types — power user, explorer, casual. Define what success looks like for each. This drives the segmentation architecture before a line of code is written.
Assign verbatim review ownership
Decide who reads the survey responses every month and how that input reaches the product team. If it is not assigned before launch, it will not happen after launch.
Design your SQS formula
Agree the composite scoring weights with your product team. The formula is less important than the agreement — everyone needs to accept the same definition of a good session.
At launch — the baseline moment
Capture your week-one baseline snapshot
Record OAR, PRR, and SQS for the first full week of live usage. This is the number everything gets measured against. Store it somewhere permanent — a dashboard, a document, a slide. It is the most important data point you will ever capture for this product.
Confirm acceptance events are firing correctly
Validate that copy, export, save, and share events are captured in QA before launch. OAR is meaningless if the acceptance signals are misfiring or missing entirely.
Set threshold alerts on OAR and PRR
Configure automatic alerts so that a 10-point drop in OAR or a 15-point rise in PRR triggers a notification to the product team without anyone having to pull a report.
30 days in — first honest look
Run your first verbatim review
Read every survey response collected in the first month. Run them through an LLM. Cluster by emotion and feature. Ask — does anything here contradict what the metrics are telling us?
Review quality scores by cohort
Compare SQS across your intent cohorts. Identify which cohort is most underserved. Make it a named priority in the next sprint planning — not a footnote.
Validate your SQS formula weights
Check whether the composite score aligns with what you are hearing in verbatims. If the score says healthy but users say struggling, the weights need adjusting.
90 days in — the improvement question
Measure improvement against the week-one baseline
Compare current OAR and PRR to the snapshot you captured at launch. This is the only honest answer to "is the product getting better?" Everything else is noise.
Review anomaly detection coverage
What percentage of metric problems were caught automatically versus reactively? If the answer is mostly reactive, the monitoring infrastructure needs investment before the next major release.
Present the Verbatim-to-Metric Gap analysis
Bring a summary to the product review showing where survey feedback and metric data aligned over the quarter — and where they diverged. The divergences are your next three product bets.
